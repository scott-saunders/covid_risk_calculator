COVID-19 Risk calculator

for Washington

2020-05-20 23:07:22

This document provides an overview of the COVID-19 cases in Washington and tries to estimate the risk of encountering infected individuals. See also the National overview and the County level reports.

Washington counties have reported 3,984 cases in the last two weeks

Since the beginning of the pandemic, Washington has reported 21,151 cases. In this report we are trying to understand how many infectious people may be circulating, so we will focus on recent cases reported in the last two weeks, 3,984. The plot below shows daily cases over time, which can help you assess whether conditions are getting better, worse, or staying about the same.

0.05% of Washington has been recently infected

To understand how prevalent cases are in the state, we need to compare the number of cases to the state population. Washington reported 3,984 cases in the last two weeks and its population is estimated to be 7,612,668. We can divide those two numbers, which tells us that 0.05% of Washington’s population has been reported infected in the last two weeks. We can perform the same calculations for every county in Washington. This is shown in the interactive map below, where the color shows the percentage of people recently infected in each county. Click on a county to open a more detailed report.

For 100 random people in Washington, we can estimate there’s a 22.9% chance 1 or more people are infected

The percent of the state that has been recently infected (0.05%) tells us the chance that any random individual is infected. But it may be more useful to think about a different question. How likely you are to encounter an infected person at the grocery store? office? apartment building? To answer this question we can calculate the probability that no one is infected in a subset of people. The remaining probability is that one or more people are infected. Please see this page for details.

Also, the reported case prevalence (0.05%) must be lower than the true number of cases. We can try to estimate the true number of cases crudely by looking at the death rate in the state. Sadly, the state reported 179 deaths in the last two weeks. If we assumed cases and deaths were constant and that the death rate of COVID-19 is 1%, then we can estimate true cases as \((\text{deaths} * 100)\). By this imperfect estimate, cases in Washington are being undercounted by 4.5x. This is not a precise estimate, but hopefully that gives you a sense for the uncertainty in the reported values.

Taking into account the potential undercount, we estimate there is a 22.9% chance 1 or more people are infected in a subset of 100 random people in Washington. Explore the plot below to see how this risk changes for different numbers of people, under 5x and 10x undercounting scenarios.

The risk varies in Washington, but it’s rarely zero.

We can see how all of the counties perform in the same test - what’s the chance that at least one person is infected in a group of 100. The plot below shows that the risk varies dramatically among counties and depending on how many cases there truly are.

What can we do?

People will make their own decisions, but there are some clear actions that we can take to minimize our risk.

  1. Continue to physically distance. The fewer people we interact with the lower the risk. Especially avoid large groups of people in tight spaces.
  2. When we must interact with people, we should assume someone is infected. That means taking reasonable precautions like wearing facemasks, washing hands, and keeping as much distance as possible.
  3. Choose lower risk situations There are higher risk contacts and lower risk contacts. This blogpost discusses some of these issues. In general, avoid close contact, indoors, with lots of people, for extended periods of time.
