To understand how prevalent cases are in the state, we need to compare the number of cases to the state population. For 100 random people in Hawaii, we can estimate there’s a 0.3% chance 1 or more people are infected

The percent of the state that has been recently infected (0.00%) tells us the chance that any random individual is infected. But it may be more useful to think about a different question. How likely you are to encounter an infected person at the grocery store? office? apartment building? To answer this question we can calculate the probability that no one is infected in a subset of people. The remaining probability is that one or more people are infected. Please see this page for details.

Also, the reported case prevalence (0.00%) must be lower than the true number of cases. We can try to estimate the true number of cases crudely by looking at the death rate in the state. Sadly, the state reported 0 deaths in the last two weeks. If we assumed cases and deaths were constant and that the death rate of COVID-19 is 1%, then we can estimate true cases as \((\text{deaths} * 100)\). By this imperfect estimate, cases in Hawaii are being undercounted by 0.0x. This is not a precise estimate, but hopefully that gives you a sense for the uncertainty in the reported values.

Taking into account the potential undercount, we estimate there is a 0.3% chance 1 or more people are infected in a subset of 100 random people in Hawaii. Explore the plot below to see how this risk changes for different numbers of people, under 5x and 10x undercounting scenarios.

What can we do?

People will make their own decisions, but there are some clear actions that we can take to minimize our risk.

  1. Continue to physically distance. The fewer people we interact with the lower the risk. Especially avoid large groups of people in tight spaces.
  2. When we must interact with people, we should assume someone is infected. That means taking reasonable precautions like wearing facemasks, washing hands, and keeping as much distance as possible.
  3. Choose lower risk situations There are higher risk contacts and lower risk contacts. This blogpost discusses some of these issues. In general, avoid close contact, indoors, with lots of people, for extended periods of time.
